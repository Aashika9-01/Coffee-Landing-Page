Our history

Coffee's history traces back to 9th-century Ethiopia, where a goat herder named Kaldi reportedly discovered its energizing effects. From there, coffee cultivation and trade spread to the Arabian Peninsula in the 15th-16th centuries, and then to Europe and Asia in the 17th century. By the 19th century, coffee had become a global commodity, and its popularity continues to this day, with countries like Brazil being major producers.

Coffee has a long and fascinating history that dates back to around the 9th century. It is believed that coffee was first discovered in Ethiopia, where, according to a popular legend, a goat herder noticed that his goats became unusually energetic after eating berries from a certain plant. These berries were later used to prepare the first form of coffee. From Ethiopia, coffee spread to Yemen in the 15th century, where it was first cultivated and used mainly by religious communities to stay awake during long prayers. Coffee gradually became popular across the Middle East, then spread to Europe in the 17th century through trade, where coffee houses became important centers for social gatherings and discussions. Later, coffee cultivation expanded to different parts of the world, especially in tropical regions. Today, countries such as Brazil are the largest producers of coffee, followed by Vietnam, Colombia, and Indonesia. Coffee plants generally grow best in warm climates with high rainfall and are mainly produced in regions known as the “Coffee Belt,” which lies between the Tropic of Cancer and the Tropic of Capricorn.
